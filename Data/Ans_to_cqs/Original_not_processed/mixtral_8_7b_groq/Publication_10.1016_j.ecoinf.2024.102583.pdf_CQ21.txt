After the training of a machine learning model for landslide susceptibility mapping (LSM), several postprocessing steps are involved. These steps are crucial for evaluating the performance of the model and understanding its predictions.

Firstly, **Metrics calculation** is performed to assess the accuracy of the model. Commonly used metrics in LSM include the Area Under the Curve (AUC) of the Receiver Operating Characteristic (ROC) curve, kappa statistics, and confusion matrix-based measures such as precision, recall, and F1-score. The AUC-ROC curve is a plot of the true positive rate against the false positive rate at different threshold values, and it provides an overall measure of the model's performance. Kappa statistics measure the agreement between the predicted and actual landslide susceptibility classes, considering the possibility of the agreement occurring by chance. Precision, recall, and F1-score are based on the confusion matrix, which is a table summarizing the number of true positives, true negatives, false positives, and false negatives.

Secondly, **Saliency maps** are used to interpret the predictions of the model. Saliency maps highlight the most important features contributing to the model's prediction for a given location. By visualizing the saliency maps, one can identify the conditioning factors that have the most significant influence on the model's prediction. This information can be used to refine the model or collect more data for the critical factors.

Thirdly, **Confusion matrix** is used to evaluate the performance of the model. It is a table that summarizes the number of true positives, true negatives, false positives, and false negatives. The confusion matrix can be used to calculate various performance metrics, such as accuracy, precision, recall, and F1-score.

Lastly, **Model interpretation techniques** such as Partial Dependence Plots (PDP) and Individual Conditional Expectation (ICE) plots are used to understand the relationship between the conditioning factors and the predicted landslide susceptibility. PDPs show the average effect of a conditioning factor on the predicted landslide susceptibility, while ICE plots show the effect of a conditioning factor for individual instances.

In conclusion, postprocessing steps such as metrics calculation, saliency maps, confusion matrix, and model interpretation techniques are essential for evaluating the performance of a machine learning model for landslide susceptibility mapping and understanding its predictions. These steps provide valuable insights into the model's behavior and can be used to refine the model or collect more data for critical factors.
